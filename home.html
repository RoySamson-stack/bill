Debunking Myths & Misconceptions

False Increase of VAT from 16% to 18%
This is false as the Finance Bill 2025 does not propose any change to the VAT rate. It remains at 16%. This confusion likely comes from earlier regional tax harmonization talks under the Medium-Term Revenue Strategy (MTRS). Advisory firms like PwC and KPMG have confirmed that the current Bill does not suggest raising the VAT rate.
Partly True All zero-rated goods will become tax-exempt
Partly true ‚Äî Some items like animal feeds, locally assembled phones, and pharmaceuticals will shift from zero-rated to exempt, which could raise their costs. However, essentials such as bread, milk, and flour will remain zero-rated.
Partly True Excise Duty will apply to all digital and imported services
Partly true ‚Äî The Bill targets specific imports like printed paper and polymer packaging, and imposes excise duty on digital services offered by non-residents through online platforms, as outlined in Sections 2 and 5.
False Landowners will be taxed 10% on idle land
This is false as the Bill does not propose a 10% tax on idle land. This claim has been widely circulated online but is not in the Bill. Always check official documents to avoid misinformation.
False 5% Agriculture tax on tea, coffee and avocado sales
This is false, as the Bill does not propose a 5% tax on the sale of tea, coffee, or avocados. The confusion likely arises from the Medium-Term Revenue Strategy (MTRS) 2023‚Äì2027, which had suggested a 5% withholding tax on agricultural produce sold through cooperatives and organized groups. However, this proposal was not adopted in the Finance Bill. While the Bill introduces changes affecting farmers, such as removing VAT exemptions on farm inputs, it does not include a direct 5% sales tax on these crops.
False PAYE will rise from 35% to 37.7%, and low earners taxed from 10% to 25%
False as the Bill does not propose these changes. The current PAYE tax rates remain the same, with 10% for low-income earners and 35% for the highest band. The misinformation likely comes from a proposal made by the World Bank in its advisory reports suggesting higher tax rates, but this recommendation was not included in the Finance Bill 2025. The current update on PAYE in the Bill is that from 1 July 2025, employers must automatically apply tax reliefs for employees.
False Mandatory KES 1,000 health insurance fee under SHIF
The bill does not introduce a mandatory KES 1,000 fee under SHIF. The KES 1,000 figure likely arises from a misinterpretation of reimbursement provisions in the Ministry of Health's tariff guidelines, which allow a KES 1,000 daily reimbursement for beneficiaries who do not complete the three-stage program or meet the 45-day minimum stay.
False New 15% tax on internet access
There is no new 15% levy on internet access services. Existing digital taxes remain at 1.5% for digital assets, and the excise duty on digital services still applies mainly to online content, gaming, and advertising. However, the Tax Laws (Amendment) Act, 2024 replaced the 1.5% Digital Service Tax with a 6% Significant Economic Presence Tax for non-residents offering services through digital marketplaces.
False Mazingira Tax will be applied broadly
This is not in the Finance Bill 2025. The KES 150 levy on packaging materials like plastics and glass comes from the Sustainable Waste Management (Extended Producer Responsibility) Regulations, 2024, enforced by NEMA under the Sustainable Waste Management Act. However, the High Court temporarily suspended these regulations on May 7, 2025, following a case filed by the Law Society of Kenya.
False Finance Bill threatens jobs and MSMEs
This is false as the Bill offers tax incentives for start-ups and investors through NIFCA ‚Äî 15% for 3 years, 20% for 4 years. It also supports MSMEs by streamlining taxes, boosting digital growth, and encouraging formalization. Paired with the MSME Policy 2025, it aims to expand funding, grow markets, and create more jobs.
False Wifi Monitoring
This is derived from the Kenya Information and Communications (Amendment) Bill 2025 which aims to shift from the current flat-rate or bundled data plans to a pay-per-use model. The Bill also proposes ISP Reporting including detailed usage data linked to meter numbers.
False Extension of the presidential limit from 5 to 7 years
The proposal is in the Constitution of Kenya (Amendment) (No. 2) Bill No. 46 of 2024, introduced in the Senate. It has faced strong public and political opposition and is in the second reading stage. For now, the presidential limit remains at two five-year terms.
False Exchange of indigenous seeds and a fine of a million shillings
This comes from the Seeds and Plant Varieties Act, which bans sharing uncertified seeds. However, the law is currently being challenged by Farmers in court, arguing it violates their rights.
False Neonatal Tax, Diaspora Remittance Tax, or 1% levy on pay bill transactions
There is no basis for claims about a Neonatal Tax, Diaspora Remittance Tax, or a 1% levy on all pay bill and till number transactions. These assertions are false and not grounded in any official policy or legislation.

Questions & Answers

How does the Bill help ordinary Kenyans?
The Bill provides multiple benefits including tax-free gratuity for retirees, increased tax-free per diems, automated tax reliefs, and protection from penalties due to system errors. It also removes VAT on essential items like electric transport and tea/coffee packaging.
What support does the Bill provide for businesses?
The Bill simplifies tax compliance, waives penalties for system errors, protects against double taxation, and reduces taxes on digital assets. It also provides clarity on tax assessments and extends time for VAT refund processing to ensure accuracy.
How does the Bill promote fairness in taxation?
The Bill ensures foreign digital businesses pay taxes like local ones, taxes luxury aviation, removes special rates for large housing developers, and requires KRA to explain assessment changes. It also prevents double penalties for tax withholding issues.
What environmental benefits does the Bill include?
The Bill exempts electric bicycles, buses, and lithium-ion batteries from VAT to promote cleaner transport. It also shifts tourism support from tax breaks to structural reforms for sustainable growth.
